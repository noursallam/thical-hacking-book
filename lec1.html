LO#01: Explain Information Security Concepts

Elements of Information Security

Confidentiality

Ensures that information is accessible only to those who are authorized to have access.

(السرية: ضمان أن المعلومات متاحة فقط لأولئك المصرح لهم بالوصول إليها)

Integrity

Maintains the trustworthiness of data or resources, preventing unauthorized or improper changes.

(النزاهة: الحفاظ على مصداقية البيانات أو الموارد، ومنع التغييرات غير المصرح بها أو غير المناسبة)

Availability

Assures that systems for delivering, storing, and processing information are accessible when required by authorized users.

(التوافر: ضمان أن أنظمة توصيل وتخزين ومعالجة المعلومات تكون قابلة للوصول عند الحاجة من قبل المستخدمين المصرح لهم)

Authenticity

Refers to the genuineness of a communication, document, or data.

(الأصالة: تشير إلى صحة الاتصال أو المستند أو البيانات)

Non-Repudiation

Guarantees that a sender cannot deny sending a message and the recipient cannot deny having received it.

(عدم الإنكار: يضمن أن المرسل لا يمكنه إنكار إرسال الرسالة وأن المستلم لا يمكنه إنكار استلامها)

Motives, Goals, and Objectives of Information Security Attacks

Formula for an Attack:

Attacks = Motive (Goal) + Method + Vulnerability

(صيغة الهجوم: الهجمات = الدافع (الهدف) + الطريقة + الثغرة)

Motive

Arises when the target system stores or processes something valuable, leading to the risk of an attack.

(الدافع: ينشأ عندما يقوم النظام الهدف بتخزين أو معالجة شيء ذي قيمة، مما يؤدي إلى خطر الهجوم)

  • Disrupting business continuity.
  • Stealing information and manipulating data.
  • Creating fear and chaos by disrupting critical infrastructures.

Method

Attackers utilize tools and techniques to exploit vulnerabilities in the system or its security controls.

(الطريقة: يستخدم المهاجمون أدوات وتقنيات لاستغلال الثغرات في النظام أو ضوابط الأمان الخاصة به)

Vulnerability

A weakness in an IT system that can be exploited by an attacker to deliver a successful attack.

(الثغرة: نقطة ضعف في نظام تكنولوجيا المعلومات يمكن للمهاجم استغلالها لتوجيه هجوم ناجح)

Classification of Attacks

1. Passive Attacks

Involve intercepting and monitoring network traffic without tampering with data.

(الهجمات السلبية: تتضمن اعتراض ومراقبة حركة المرور على الشبكة دون العبث بالبيانات)

2. Active Attacks

Tamper with data in transit, disrupt communication, or services between systems.

(الهجمات النشطة: العبث بالبيانات أثناء النقل، تعطيل الاتصال أو الخدمات بين الأنظمة)

LO#02: Explain Hacking Methodologies and Frameworks

Phases of Hacking

1. Footprinting

Gathering information about the target.

(جمع المعلومات عن الهدف)

2. Scanning

Probing the target system for open ports and vulnerabilities.

(فحص النظام الهدف للبحث عن المنافذ المفتوحة والثغرات)

3. Enumeration

Discovering resources and services on the target system.

(الحصول على المصدرات والخدمات في نظام الهدف)

4. Exploitation

Exploiting vulnerabilities to gain access to the target system.

(التصرف في الثغرات للحصول على الوصول لنظام الهدف)

System Hacking Steps

  1. Gaining Access:
    • Cracking passwords.
    • Exploiting vulnerabilities.
  2. Escalating Privileges:
    • Increasing access rights to gain more control over the system.
  3. Maintaining Access:
    • Executing applications to sustain a presence on the system.
    • Hiding files to avoid detection.
  4. Clearing Logs:
    • Covering tracks to erase evidence of intrusion.

Cyber Kill Chain Framework

A framework used for understanding and preventing malicious intrusion activities. It provides insights into an attacker's tactics, techniques, and procedures.

  1. Reconnaissance: Gathering data about the target to identify weak points.
  2. Weaponization: Creating a deliverable malicious payload using an exploit and a backdoor.
  3. Delivery: Sending the weaponized payload to the victim via email, USB, or other methods.
  4. Exploitation: Executing malicious code on the victim’s system to exploit vulnerabilities.
  5. Installation: Installing malware or other malicious tools on the target system.
  6. Command and Control (C2): Establishing a channel to communicate with the compromised system and pass data.
  7. Actions on Objectives: Executing the intended actions, such as stealing data, disrupting services, or achieving other malicious goals.

Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs)

Patterns of activities and methods used by threat actors. TTPs are key to understanding the behavior of attackers.

  • Tactics:
    • Guidelines that describe how an attacker performs an attack from start to finish.
    • Includes various tactics for information gathering, initial exploitation, privilege escalation, lateral movement, and persistent access deployment.
  • Techniques:
    • Technical methods used by attackers to achieve specific results during an attack.
    • Examples include initial exploitation, setting up and maintaining command and control channels, accessing the target infrastructure, and covering tracks or data exfiltration.
  • Procedures:
    • Organizational approaches threat actors follow to execute an attack.
    • The number of actions varies based on the goals and objectives of the attackers.

Implications of Hacking

  • Unauthorized Access: Leads to breaches of sensitive data.
  • Damage to Applications: Alteration or misuse of software functionalities.
  • Business Risks: Financial loss, reputation damage, or operational disruption.

Who is a Hacker?

A hacker is an intelligent individual with excellent computer skills who can create and explore computer software, understand and manipulate hardware. Hacking can be a hobby, a test of skills, or an exploration of how many computers or networks they can compromise.

  • Motivations:
    • Gaining knowledge about systems.
    • Probing systems for weaknesses.
    • Engaging in illegal activities such as stealing data.
  • Malicious Intent:
    • Stealing business data, credit card information, or social security numbers.
    • Obtaining email passwords and other sensitive data.

Hacker Classes

  • Black Hats: Hackers with extraordinary skills who engage in malicious or destructive activities. Often referred to as crackers. Intent: Exploitation and harm.
  • White Hats: Also known as ethical hackers or security analysts. Use their skills for defensive purposes with permission from system owners. Intent: Protection and security.
  • Gray Hats: Hackers who operate both offensively and defensively at different times. Their actions may not always align with the law. Intent: Mixed motives.
  • Suicide Hackers: Aim to bring down critical infrastructure for a cause. Unconcerned about consequences such as imprisonment. Intent: Ideological disruption.
  • Script Kiddies: Unskilled hackers relying on pre-written scripts and tools developed by others. Lack the technical expertise of experienced hackers. Intent: Often for fun or nuisance.
  • Cyber Terrorists: Use hacking to further religious or political agendas. Create fear by causing large-scale disruptions to networks. Intent: Instill fear and chaos.
  • State-Sponsored Hackers: Employed by governments to gain top-secret information or damage other nations’ systems. Often involved in espionage or cyber warfare. Intent: National interests.
  • Hacktivists: Promote a political agenda using hacking. Activities include defacing or disabling websites to spread their message. Intent: Advocacy or protest.
  • Hacker Teams: Groups of skilled hackers with their own resources and funding. Work together to research and develop state-of-the-art technologies. Intent: Collaborative exploration and innovation, sometimes for malicious purposes.
